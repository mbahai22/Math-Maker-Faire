3D Fractal Geometry and Mandelbrot


What Is It?

According to the Fractal Foundation, a fractal is "an infinitely complex pattern that is self-similar across different scales," or in other words a shape that constantly repeats in shape and structure when zoomed in on. These phenomenan are found all over the natural world but more complex fractals can be found in the mathematical world. For example, one fractal is the mandelbrot set (right).

Who is Mandelbrot?

Benoit Mandelbrot was a French, Polish, and American mathematician who was born on November 20, 1984, to a family of immigrants from Warsaw, who later moved to France. At a young age, Mandelbrot studied mathematics in school and he grew up to work at IBM and act as a professor at Harvard. He later revolutionized the feild of fractal geometry both in the mathematical sense as well as in the natural world, as he pointed out how many of the many phenomenon of the world like clouds and shorelines, had an order to their chaotic structure.

The Mandelbrot set

The mandelbrot set is one of the most famous examples of fractals in the mathematical world and it has the equation: zn+1 = zn^2 + c. The c and z variables of the equation consist of complex numbers and the n variable acts as a positive integer. According to LiveScience, complex numbers are numbers that consist of real numbers and imaginary numbers, which are numbers that equate to the square root of a negative number, these complex numbers are most notably described as a+bi, a being real numbers, and i being imaginary numbers. In order to cause for this fractal to take shape, the equation uses iteration (a completely repetitive process. In order to learn more about this process you can look to this webpage, which goes more in depth onto the function of this equation. However, to put it simply, we must plug in seperate numbers and see the results that they project, if the input repeats exponentially, it will depict a certain color that is seperate from the mandelbrot set, however, if there is a form of repetition in the output of these numbers, they weill fall under our equation and start to form the mandelbrot set. This equation's use of iteration and repetition, mixed with Mandelbrot's chaos theory causes for this set to be one of the most important fractals and geometrical discoveries of the known world.
